Dataset Architecture & Methodology
Every metric in this document is produced by a live intelligence pipeline, not a static dataset.
The analysis that follows is generated from STOC's Corporate Development Support (CDS) intelligence layer. Each target in the dataset is scored, classified, and refreshed continuously. What follows defines what that means in practice.
STOC's core dataset of 12,920 licensed medical spas sits within a broader universe of U.S. locations offering medical-grade aesthetic services. For context, the American Med Spa Association enumerates approximately 10,488 licensed medical spas (AmSpa, 2024). The difference is intentional. STOC maps all U.S. locations offering medical-grade aesthetic services under physician supervision, including standalone medical spas, cosmetic dermatology practices with aesthetic service lines, and plastic surgery clinics with non-surgical revenue. This broader scope reflects how PE platforms actually build acquisitions - routinely including adjacent practice types that operate economically as med spas regardless of licensure classification.

Geographic Intelligence
PE Concentrated in Major Metros. 7,156 Mid-Density Targets Were Largely Untouched.
Three density tiers define how this market is structured. PE capital concentrated in Dense Urban markets where scores were highest - and in doing so, left an uncontested pool of 7,156 Mid-Density targets largely untouched. Those markets score only 8 points lower than major metros. The PE pressure is 40% less. The acquisition economics have not moved yet.
Dense Urban  4,515 targets
Major metro and core urban markets. Population 50K+. Z1: 0-2 mi  ·  Z2: 2-5 mi  ·  Z3: 5-10 mi  ·  Z4: 10-15 mi. Max cap 15 miles applied to dense metros (NYC, Boston, DC, SF) to prevent overstatement of tuck-in range.
Mid-Density  7,156 targets
Secondary and mid-sized markets. Generally 15K+. Z1: 0-3 mi  ·  Z2: 3-7 mi  ·  Z3: 7-15 mi  ·  Z4: 15-25 mi. Radius assumptions best calibrated to actual competitive structure at this tier.
Suburban  1,249 targets
Smaller surrounding and non-core markets below mid-density threshold. Z1: 0-5 mi  ·  Z2: 5-10 mi  ·  Z3: 10-20 mi  ·  Z4: 20-30 mi. Wider radius required to avoid misreading sparse supply as whitespace.

Major metro markets score well because they have competition, volume, and digital maturity. PE recognized that and entered early. Sellers have not had the transaction conversation. Prices reflect operations, not platform multiples. CDS identifies and sequences outreach into these markets before that changes.

The Pre-Consolidation Window
The 0% bucket is not simply undiscovered territory. These are markets with sufficient target density, verified supply gaps, and no incumbent PE pressure. Entry economics in a 0% CBSA are structurally different from a 6-15% CBSA: seller expectations are lower, multiples are uncompressed, and de novo remains a viable complement to acquisition. Untouched CBSAs score highest at 71.7. Once PE enters a market, average STOC scores compress into a narrower 55.8 to 58.3 range across PE-entered buckets. Zero-PE CBSAs score 14.1 points higher than PE-entered markets overall (71.7 vs. a combined PE-entered average of 57.6).
First-Mover Implication
In 23.1% of tracked CBSAs (148 of 640), PE entry has begun compressing multiples. This analysis measures current PE penetration by market, not the pace of entry over time. CDS tracks competitive arrival in real time across all 640 markets, flagging which 0% CBSAs are receiving inbound sponsor interest before a transaction closes.
